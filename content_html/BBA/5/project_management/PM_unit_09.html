UNIT 9: Social Cost Benefit Analysis (SCBA)

1. SOCIAL COST BENEFIT ANALYSIS (SCBA)

SCBA is an economic evaluation technique used to appraise projects from the viewpoint of the entire society or economy, rather than solely from the perspective of the investing organization.

It involves systematically identifying, measuring, and valuing all costs and benefits—including those external effects (externalities) that do not appear in conventional financial accounts—to determine the net contribution of the project to national welfare.

2. RATIONALE FOR SCBA

SCBA is necessary because the market prices used in financial analysis are often distorted and do not reflect the true scarcity value or opportunity cost to society.

Reasons for Market Price Distortion:

  1. Taxes and Subsidies: Taxes (like GST) inflate prices, while subsidies deflate them, meaning neither reflects the actual economic cost of production.
  2. Externalities: Projects create costs (like pollution, noise) or benefits (like improved infrastructure, reduced traffic congestion) for third parties that are not compensated or paid for.
  3. Market Imperfections: Monopoly power, price controls, and unemployment (especially of unskilled labor) prevent market prices from reflecting true social opportunity costs.

3. SHADOW PRICING (ACCOUNTING PRICES)

Shadow Prices (or Accounting Prices) are the true economic value of a good, service, or resource, adjusted to reflect its actual scarcity value or opportunity cost to the society, net of all market distortions.

  • Objective: To replace distorted market prices with prices that truly reflect the social value and cost of resources.
  • Application: In SCBA, a shadow price is used for calculating the cost/benefit instead of the observed market price.

Shadow Pricing for Key Factors:

  1. Foreign Exchange: The market exchange rate is often controlled or distorted. The shadow price of foreign exchange is its true social cost, reflecting the value of imports saved or exports generated.
  2. Labour: Due to high unemployment, the social opportunity cost of unskilled labour (the lost output from alternative employment) is often lower than the market wage rate. Using a lower shadow price encourages employment-generating projects.
  3. Capital: The social cost of capital (Social Discount Rate, SDR) is used instead of the market interest rate (which is distorted by taxes and risk), reflecting society's time preference for consumption.

4. UNIDO APPROACH TO SCBA (United Nations Industrial Development Organization)

The UNIDO approach provides a five-step framework for project evaluation by systematically adjusting financial values to arrive at social values.

StageFocus of AnalysisKey Concept
Stage 1: Financial Profitability (Market Prices)Calculate profitability from the commercial (firm’s) viewpoint.Uses unadjusted market prices.
Stage 2: Economic/SCBA Profitability (Shadow Prices)Recalculate profitability from the national (society's) viewpoint.Replace all market prices with Shadow Prices.
Stage 3: Adjustments for ExternalitiesIdentify and value all social costs (e.g., pollution, displacement) and social benefits (e.g., training, infrastructure) not included in financial accounts.Quantify and value Externalities.
Stage 4: Adjustments for Savings and InvestmentAccount for the social value of funds, as society values a rupee invested more than a rupee consumed.Determine the Consumption vs. Investment social weights.
Stage 5: Adjustments for Income DistributionExamine how the project's benefits and costs are distributed across different income groups (e.g., rich vs. poor).Apply Distributional Weights to favor benefits accruing to the poor.

The final result is the Net Social Benefit (NSB), which determines the project's worth to the nation.

5. PUBLIC SECTOR INVESTMENT IN INDIA

SCBA is most directly and crucially applied to Public Sector Investment in India. Public sector projects (like metro rail, dams, or public health initiatives) are typically undertaken not for profit maximization, but for the maximization of social welfare.

  • Justification of Public Projects: Projects are justified based on their Net Social Benefit (NSB).
  • Shadow Pricing for Labour: The social cost of using unskilled labour is often valued lower than its market wage to incentivize projects that generate significant employment, making job creation a social benefit.
  • Distributional Weights: SCBA helps ensure that large infrastructure projects do not disproportionately burden the poor while benefiting the wealthy, thereby addressing social equity goals.
