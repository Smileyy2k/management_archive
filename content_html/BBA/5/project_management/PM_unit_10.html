UNIT 10: Project Management Processes

1. PROJECT PLANNING

1.1 Meaning and Objectives

Meaning of Planning: The process of establishing project objectives, identifying the activities required to achieve them, determining the resources needed, and defining the sequence and schedule of these activities.

Objectives: To provide a roadmap for execution, ensure the optimal allocation of resources, identify potential risks, establish a baseline for performance measurement, and align all stakeholders.

1.2 Process of Planning (The Project Management Plan)

The planning process is iterative and includes defining the scope, schedule, and cost baselines.

  1. Defining Scope: Determining the project boundaries and deliverables.
  2. Creating the Work Breakdown Structure (WBS): Decomposing the major deliverables into smaller, manageable tasks.
  3. Activity Definition: Identifying the specific tasks needed to produce the deliverables.
  4. Sequence and Dependency Determination: Establishing the order in which tasks must be performed.
  5. Resource Estimation: Determining the types and quantities of resources (people, equipment, materials) needed.
  6. Time Estimation: Forecasting the duration of each activity.
  7. Schedule Development: Creating the project timetable (e.g., using Gantt Charts, Critical Path Method).
  8. Cost Estimation and Budgeting: Determining the total budget and allocating funds to tasks.
  9. Risk Planning: Developing strategies to handle identified risks (Mitigate, Avoid, Transfer, Accept).

2. PROJECT EXECUTION

The process of performing the work defined in the Project Management Plan to achieve the project objectives. This phase consumes most of the project budget and resources.

  • Direct and Manage Project Work: Carrying out the planned activities (e.g., construction, coding, testing).
  • Managing Project Team: Acquiring, developing, and managing the team members.
  • Managing Communications and Stakeholders: Ensuring timely collection, creation, distribution, storage, and ultimate disposition of project information.
  • Quality Assurance: Auditing the quality requirements and results from quality control measurements to ensure appropriate quality standards are used.
  • Procurement Management: Managing the contracts and relationships with external vendors/suppliers.

3. PROJECT MONITORING AND CONTROL

The process of tracking, reviewing, and regulating the progress and performance of the project; identifying any areas in which the plan requires changes; and initiating the corresponding changes.

Key Monitoring and Control Activities

  1. Performance Measurement: Comparing actual performance against the baselines (Scope, Schedule, Cost).
  2. Earned Value Management (EVM): A method that integrates scope, schedule, and cost to assess project performance.
    • PV (Planned Value): Budgeted cost of work scheduled.
    • EV (Earned Value): Budgeted cost of work performed.
    • AC (Actual Cost): Actual cost of work performed.
  3. Variance Analysis: Calculating schedule variance (SV = EV - PV) and cost variance (CV = EV - AC).
  4. Change Control: Managing and approving/rejecting all change requests (scope, schedule, budget) through a formal Integrated Change Control process.
  5. Risk Monitoring: Tracking residual and secondary risks and executing planned response strategies.

4. PROJECT CLOSURE

The process of finalizing all activities across all process groups to formally complete the project, phase, or contract and formally transfer the project's output.

7.2 Methods of Project Termination

  1. Termination by Extinction: The project is completed (successfully or unsuccessfully) or suddenly stopped. The work is done, or the project is deemed a failure/no longer necessary.
  2. Termination by Addition: The project is successfully completed, and its output (e.g., a new product division or system) is absorbed into the parent organization as a permanent functional unit.
  3. Termination by Integration: The project's resources (people, systems) are simply distributed back into the existing functional units of the parent organization.

7.3 Process of Project Termination

The formal closure process ensures a clean and well-documented close:

  • Completion of Deliverables: Ensuring all scope elements are finalized and formally accepted by the customer.
  • Contract Closure: Finalizing all administrative and contractual agreements with vendors and suppliers.
  • Final Report: Documenting performance against baselines, analyzing variances, and confirming customer sign-off.
  • Lessons Learned: Conducting a post-mortem review to document what worked well and what could be improved for future projects.
  • Releasing Resources: Disbanding the project team and reassigning resources to other functional areas or projects.
