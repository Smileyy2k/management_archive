UNIT 4: Technical Analysis

1. PROJECT PROCUREMENT MANAGEMENT

Project procurement management includes the processes necessary to purchase or acquire products, services, or results needed from outside the project team to perform the work.

Make-or-Buy Analysis

This is a fundamental decision-making process where the project team determines whether specific work or deliverables should be produced internally (Make) or purchased from external vendors (Buy).

Make (Internal Production) Factors:

  • Cost Considerations: Compare internal production costs vs. external purchase costs.
  • Quality Control: Desire for better control over quality standards.
  • Intellectual Property: Need to protect proprietary knowledge and processes.
  • Strategic Capability: Develop or maintain internal capabilities that are strategic.
  • Capacity Utilization: Use available internal capacity effectively.

Buy (External Procurement) Factors:

  • Cost Efficiency: Suppliers may have cost advantages through specialization and economies of scale.
  • Expertise Access: Access to specialized skills and technologies not available internally.
  • Focus on Core Business: Concentrate internal resources on core competencies.
  • Risk Transfer: Transfer certain risks (e.g., performance, schedule) to suppliers.
  • Scalability: Easier to scale up or down based on demand.

2. TECHNICAL ANALYSIS: PLANT CAPACITY AND LOCATION

Plant Capacity

Plant capacity is the maximum sustainable output rate a production facility can achieve. It's crucial because it affects operating efficiency, cost structure, and the project's ability to meet projected demand.

Types of Capacity:

  1. Installed/Rated Capacity: The maximum output rate a plant is designed to achieve under ideal conditions. (Theoretical maximum).
  2. Normal/Practical Capacity: The realistic maximum output rate achievable, considering planned maintenance, normal delays, and expected inefficiencies. (Practical limit).
  3. Achieved Capacity: The actual output rate obtained over a specific period. (Actual output).

The technical analysis must ensure the Normal/Practical Capacity is sufficient to meet the Projected Demand identified in the market analysis.

Factors Influencing Plant Location

Selecting the optimal plant location involves balancing various cost, market, and environmental factors.

  1. Proximity to Raw Materials: Minimizes transport costs for bulky or perishable materials.
  2. Proximity to Market: Reduces costs and time for delivering finished goods to customers (crucial for fragile or high-volume products).
  3. Availability of Infrastructure: Access to reliable power, water supply, communication links, and transport networks (roads, rail, ports).
  4. Availability of Labour: Presence of skilled/unskilled labor pool at reasonable wage rates.
  5. Government Policies and Incentives: Tax breaks, subsidies, or relaxed regulations in specific zones (e.g., SEZs, backward areas).
  6. Environmental and Regulatory Factors: Compliance with local pollution control laws and ease of getting environmental clearance.

3. TECHNOLOGY AND EQUIPMENT SELECTION

Technology Selection Criteria

  1. Appropriateness: Is the technology the most suitable for the scale and quality requirements of the project?
  2. Cost-Effectiveness: Comparing the total cost of ownership (purchase, installation, maintenance, and operating costs) of different technologies.
  3. Scalability and Flexibility: Can the technology be easily scaled up/down? Can it handle variations in product mix?
  4. Environmental Impact: Does the technology minimize waste and pollution?
  5. Local Availability: Is the technology and its spare parts readily available and maintainable by local technicians?
  6. Track Record: Has the technology been proven successful in similar operating environments?

Equipment Selection

Equipment selection focuses on specific machinery required for the chosen technology.

  • Technical Specifications: Matching capacity, precision, and speed requirements.
  • Supplier Reliability: Assessing the vendor's reputation, warranty, and after-sales service.
  • Operating Efficiency: Energy consumption and maintenance costs.
  • Residual Value: Estimating the resale value of the equipment at the end of its useful life.

4. PROJECT SCHEDULING AND PRECEDENCE DIAGRAMMING

Project Scheduling

Project scheduling is the process of creating a timeline for the project by identifying activities, estimating their duration, and sequencing them to determine the total time required for completion.

Activity Dependency Types (Precedence Relationships)

These relationships define the logical order in which activities must be performed. The two primary types are:

  1. Finish-to-Start (FS): Activity B cannot start until Activity A finishes. (Most common).
    • Example: Painting (B) cannot start until wall construction (A) finishes.
  2. Start-to-Start (SS): Activity B can only start once Activity A has started.
    • Example: Drafting the final report (B) can start as soon as data analysis (A) starts.
  3. Finish-to-Finish (FF): Activity B cannot finish until Activity A finishes.
    • Example: System testing (B) cannot finish until all coding modules (A) are finished.
  4. Start-to-Finish (SF): Activity B finishes when Activity A starts. (Very rare).
    • Example: Training the replacement team (B) must finish the day the old team hands over (A).

Precedence Diagramming Method (PDM) / Activity-on-Node (AON)

Activities are represented as nodes (boxes), and the relationships (dependencies) between them are shown as arrows. This is the most widely used method for project network diagrams.
