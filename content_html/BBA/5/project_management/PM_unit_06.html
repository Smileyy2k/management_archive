UNIT 6: Appraisal Criteria

1. WHAT IS PROJECT APPRAISAL?

Project Appraisal is the systematic and comprehensive analysis of an investment proposal (a project) to determine its viability, sustainability, and alignment with the organization's strategic objectives before resources are committed.

It involves evaluating various facets of the project, including financial, economic, technical, managerial, and social aspects.

Objectives of Appraisal:

  • To select the most profitable or strategically important projects from multiple alternatives.
  • To allocate scarce resources (like capital and skilled labour) efficiently.
  • To ensure the project aligns with the company's long-term goals.

2. DEFINING CASH FLOW (CF)

Cash flow is the movement of money into (inflows) and out of (outflows) a business or project over a specific period. For project appraisal, it is crucial to define which cash flows are relevant based on the perspective.

A. Cash Flow from Long-Term Equity (Shareholders' Point of View)

This perspective focuses on the funds remaining for equity holders after all other obligations have been met.

Relevant Cash Flow: Profit After Tax (PAT) + Depreciation - Investment in Fixed Assets - Repayment of Debt + New Borrowings.

B. Cash Flow from Long-Term Lenders (Creditors' Point of View)

This perspective focuses on the return to the lending institutions.

Relevant Cash Flow: Interest Received + Principal Repayment Received.

C. Cash Flow for the Project as a Whole (Total Capital View)

This perspective ignores the source of funds (debt vs. equity) and is the most common for capital budgeting.

Relevant Cash Flow: Profit After Tax (PAT) + Depreciation + Interest (1-Tax Rate) - Investment in Fixed Assets.

3. APPRAISAL CRITERIA: DISCOUNTED CASH FLOW (DCF) TECHNIQUES

DCF methods recognize the Time Value of Money (TVM), meaning a rupee received today is worth more than a rupee received tomorrow. This is done by discounting future cash flows back to the present using a discount rate (usually the Cost of Capital, WACC).

i. Net Present Value (NPV)

NPV is the sum of the present values (PV) of all project cash inflows minus the sum of the present values of all cash outflows. The initial investment is a cash outflow at time zero.

$$ \text{NPV} = \sum_{t=1}^{n} \frac{\text{Cash Flow}_t}{(1+k)^t} - \text{Initial Investment} $$

  • Decision Rule: Accept the project if NPV > 0. Reject if NPV < 0.
  • Selection Rule (Mutually Exclusive Projects): Select the project with the highest positive NPV.
  • Merit: Provides the theoretical change in wealth (in today's rupees) resulting from the project.

ii. Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

IRR is the discount rate (r) that makes the project's Net Present Value (NPV) equal to zero. It represents the expected rate of return the project will generate.

$$ \sum_{t=1}^{n} \frac{\text{Cash Flow}_t}{(1+\text{IRR})^t} - \text{Initial Investment} = 0 $$

  • Decision Rule: Accept the project if IRR > Cost of Capital ($k$). Reject if IRR < k.
  • Merit: Easy to understand and communicate to non-financial managers (it's a rate of return).

iii. Profitability Index (PI) / Benefit-Cost Ratio

PI is the ratio of the present value of future cash inflows to the present value of cash outflows (or Initial Investment). It measures the benefit per rupee of cost.

$$ \text{PI} = \frac{\text{Present Value of Cash Inflows}}{\text{Present Value of Cash Outflows}} $$

  • Decision Rule: Accept the project if PI > 1. Reject if PI < 1.
  • Merit: Useful for ranking projects when there is a capital rationing constraint.

4. APPRAISAL CRITERIA: NON-DCF TECHNIQUES

These methods do not consider the Time Value of Money (TVM), making them less accurate for long-term projects but simpler to calculate.

i. Payback Period (PBP)

The time period required for the cumulative cash inflows from the project to equal the initial investment.

  • Decision Rule: Accept the project if its PBP is less than a pre-determined maximum payback period set by management.
  • Merit: Simple to calculate; measures liquidity and risk exposure (shorter PBP means faster recovery of funds).
  • Demerit: Ignores cash flows occurring after the payback period; ignores TVM.

ii. Accounting Rate of Return (ARR) / Average Rate of Return

ARR expresses the average annual accounting profit generated by a project as a percentage of the average or initial investment.

$$ \text{ARR} = \frac{\text{Average Annual Net Income}}{\text{Average Investment}} \times 100 $$

  • Decision Rule: Accept the project if ARR is greater than a pre-determined target rate.
  • Demerit: Uses accounting profit (not cash flow); ignores TVM.

5. HANDLING COMPLEX APPRAISAL ISSUES

i. Projects with Unequal Lives

When comparing mutually exclusive projects with different operational lives, simple NPV comparison is flawed. The standard solution is:

Solution: Use the Equivalent Annual Annuity (EAA) method.

  • EAA converts the NPV of a project into an equal annual cash flow equivalent over its life.
  • Decision Rule: Select the project with the higher EAA.

ii. Projects Subject to Capital Rationing

This occurs when a firm has profitable projects (NPV > 0) but lacks sufficient funds to invest in all of them.

Solution: Use the Profitability Index (PI).

  • Decision Rule: Rank projects by their PI and choose the highest-ranking projects until the available capital is exhausted.
