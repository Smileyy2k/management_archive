UNIT 5: Financial Projections

1. COST OF THE PROJECT

Project cost is the total expenditure required to implement and operate the project until it reaches commercial production or operational status. It includes all capital and pre-operative expenses.

Project Cost Components

  1. **Capital Expenditure (CAPEX):** Investment in long-term assets.
    • **Fixed Assets:** Land, building, plant & machinery, equipment.
    • **Intangible Assets:** Software, patents, licenses, preliminary expenses.
  2. **Working Capital:** Funds needed for day-to-day operations.
    • **Net Working Capital** = Current Assets (Raw materials, finished goods, debtors, cash) - Current Liabilities (Creditors, accrued expenses).
  3. **Pre-operative Expenses:** Expenses incurred before commercial operations begin.
    • Feasibility studies, legal fees, trial runs, training costs, interest during construction period.
  4. **Contingency (5-10% of total cost):** An allocation for unforeseen expenses, price escalations, or minor design changes.

Cost Estimation Methods

Techniques used to predict the cost of a project, varying in accuracy and detail.

  1. **Analogous Estimation (Top-Down):** Uses data from similar, completed past projects to estimate the current project's cost.
    • **Pros:** Quick, used in early stages when information is limited.
    • **Cons:** Less accurate due to potential differences between projects.
  2. **Parametric Estimation:** Uses a **statistical relationship** between historical data and other variables (e.g., cost per square foot, cost per line of code) to calculate an estimate.
    • **Pros:** Highly accurate if the relationship is reliable and scale is a key factor.
    • **Cons:** Requires good historical data and a quantifiable variable.
  3. **Bottom-Up Estimation:** The most detailed method, where the cost of individual work packages is estimated and then aggregated to get the total project cost.
    • **Pros:** Most accurate and detailed.
    • **Cons:** Time-consuming and requires a fully defined Work Breakdown Structure (WBS).

2. SOURCES OF FINANCE

Financial sources are broadly categorized into internal and external, and equity and debt.

Internal Sources

  • **Retained Earnings:** Profits kept in the business rather than paid out as dividends.
  • **Sale of Assets:** Disposal of non-core or surplus assets.
  • **Depreciation Provision:** Non-cash expense that generates internal funds for replacement.

External Sources

  1. **Equity:** Funds raised by issuing ownership shares.
    • **Ordinary Shares/Common Stock:** Permanent capital with voting rights.
    • **Preference Shares:** Fixed dividend, priority in repayment, no/limited voting rights.
  2. **Debt:** Borrowed funds that must be repaid with interest.
    • **Term Loans:** Long-term funding from commercial banks or financial institutions.
    • **Debentures/Bonds:** Secured or unsecured debt instruments issued to the public.
    • **Working Capital Finance:** Short-term loans like cash credit, overdraft, or trade credit.
  3. **Hybrid Finance:** Instruments that combine features of both debt and equity (e.g., convertible debentures, venture capital).

3. PROJECTED FINANCIAL STATEMENTS

1. Projected Income Statement (Profit & Loss)

This statement projects the project's profitability over a period by matching revenues and expenses.

ComponentDescription
**Sales Revenue**Projected volume × Unit Price
**Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)**Direct costs like raw materials, labor, and factory overhead.
**Gross Profit**Sales Revenue - COGS
**Operating Expenses**Selling, General, and Administrative (SG&A) costs.
**EBIT (Earnings Before Interest & Tax)**Gross Profit - Operating Expenses
**Interest Expense**Cost of debt (loans, bonds).
**PBT (Profit Before Tax)**EBIT - Interest
**Net Profit (PAT)**PBT - Tax

2. Projected Balance Sheet (Statement of Financial Position)

A snapshot of the project’s assets, liabilities, and equity at a specific point in time, following the accounting identity: **Assets = Liabilities + Equity**.

  • **Assets:** What the project owns (Fixed Assets, Current Assets).
  • **Liabilities:** What the project owes to outsiders (Debt, Creditors).
  • **Equity:** The owners' stake (Share Capital, Retained Earnings).

3. Projected Cash Flow Statement (CFS)

This statement tracks the actual movement of cash (inflows and outflows) during a period, categorized into three sections.

  1. **Operating Cash Flow (OCF):** Cash generated from normal business activities (e.g., cash from sales, cash paid for materials).
  2. **Investment Cash Flow (ICF):** Cash used for acquiring or selling long-term assets (e.g., Capital Expenditure on machinery, sale of old assets).
  3. **Financing Cash Flow (FCF):** Cash related to raising funds and paying capital providers (e.g., Loan Receipts, Repayments, Equity Contributions, Dividend Payments).
