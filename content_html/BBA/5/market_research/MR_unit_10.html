UNIT 10: OBSERVATION AND PHYSIOLOGICAL MEASURES

1. WHAT IS OBSERVATION

Observation is the systematic process of recording the behavioral patterns of people, objects, and events without directly questioning or communicating with the subjects. Instead of asking consumers why they bought a product, observation involves silently watching what they actually do (e.g., how long they spend looking at a display, the path they take through a store).

2. BEHAVIORAL AND NON-BEHAVIORAL OBSERVATION

Observation can be classified based on whether the observer is watching actions or recorded states.

Type Focus Examples
Behavioral Observation Watching actions, body language, nonverbal cues, and spatial relationships. A child opening a cereal box; a consumer frowning at a price tag; the time taken to complete online checkout.
Non-behavioral Observation Recording inanimate objects, past analytical records, or physical conditions. Counting inventory in a store; checking the quality of a road surface; counting brand logo appearances in a movie.

Observation can also be classified by method:

  • Structured vs. Unstructured:
    • Structured: The researcher clearly defines what to observe and how to record it (e.g., using a checklist). Used when the problem is clearly defined.
    • Unstructured: The observer records all relevant phenomena without pre-defined rules. Used in exploratory research.
  • Natural vs. Contrived:
    • Natural: Observing behavior as it takes place in its natural environment (e.g., watching shoppers in a grocery store).
    • Contrived: The researcher creates an artificial environment to observe behavior (e.g., setting up a mock store or lab).

3. EVALUATION OF OBSERVATIONAL METHOD

Category Advantages Disadvantages
Accuracy Eliminates response bias and social desirability bias. Cannot observe underlying motivations, beliefs, or attitudes (the "why").
Data Quality Provides accurate data on behavior, especially for unconscious actions. Observation is selective; infrequent or short-duration behaviors might be missed.
Time & Cost Can be faster and cheaper than surveying if the behavior is common. Can be very time-consuming and expensive if the behavior is rare.
Depth The only way to record behavior when the subject is unable to report it (e.g., children). Ethical concerns, especially when using hidden techniques.

4. THE OBSERVER-SUBJECT RELATION

This refers to whether the subject is aware they are being observed, which dictates how natural their behavior will be.

Relation Type Term Explanation
Open (Known) Undisguised Observation The subjects are aware they are being observed. This may lead to the Hawthorne Effect (subjects change behavior because they know they are being watched).
Hidden (Unknown) Disguised Observation Subjects are unaware that observation is taking place (e.g., hidden cameras). This leads to more natural behavior but raises serious ethical concerns.

5. CONDUCTING AN OBSERVATIONAL STUDY

The process for designing and executing an observational study:

  1. Define the Objectives: What specific behavior needs to be measured?
  2. Select the Method: Choose between human or mechanical/electronic observation.
  3. Create the Observation Form: Develop a structured document or checklist.
  4. Select the Site/Sample: Determine the location and time for the observation.
  5. Train the Observers: Ensure human observers are highly consistent in how they record data.
  6. Data Collection and Analysis: Execute the observation and analyze the recorded data.

6. UNOBTRUSIVE MEASURES

Unobtrusive measures are methods of data collection that involve no direct interaction with the subjects. They are entirely disguised and typically rely on physical traces or archival data. They are often used to measure the results of past behavior.

Examples:

  • Garbage Analysis (Physical Traces): Looking at household trash to determine actual consumption patterns.
  • Erosion: Measuring the wear on floor tiles in a museum to determine the popularity of exhibits.
  • Archival Records: Analyzing company sales data or public records.

7. PHYSIOLOGICAL MEASURES

Physiological Measures are mechanical or electronic recordings of involuntary biological and physical reactions of consumers to marketing stimuli. These tools measure subconscious changes in the body that indicate interest, arousal, or emotional response, bypassing a person's ability to lie. This area is often called Neuromarketing.

Measure What is Measured What it Indicates
Eye Tracking Eye fixation and pupil dilation. Fixation: Attention and interest. Dilation: Arousal, cognitive effort, or emotional response.
Galvanic Skin Response (GSR) Electrical resistance of the skin (sweating). Emotional Arousal (excitement, tension, shock, or high engagement).
Electroencephalography (EEG) Electrical activity in the brain (brain waves). Engagement, memory formation, and relative emotional valence (positive/negative response).
Facial Coding Analysis of micro-expressions (subtle, involuntary facial muscle movements). Specific emotions like joy, disgust, or surprise, often used to evaluate ads.
