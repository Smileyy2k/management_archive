1. INTRODUCTION TO RESEARCH PROPOSAL AND DESIGN

What is a Research Proposal?

A research proposal is a detailed plan that outlines what research will be conducted, how it will be done, why it's important, and what resources are needed. Think of it as a blueprint for a building - it shows exactly what will be constructed, how, and with what materials.

2. BASIC AND APPLIED RESEARCH

BASIC RESEARCH (PURE RESEARCH)

Research conducted to increase fundamental knowledge and understanding without immediate commercial application.

  • Focuses on theory building
  • Long-term orientation
  • Academic in nature
  • Contributes to scientific knowledge

Example: A study on "How do colors affect emotional responses to advertisements?" Its purpose is understanding the fundamental relationship between color and emotion, which may later inform advertising design.

APPLIED RESEARCH (PRACTICAL RESEARCH)

Research conducted to solve specific, immediate problems or to make decisions in real-world situations.

  • Problem-solving oriented
  • Short-term focus
  • Commercial/business applications
  • Action-oriented outcomes

Example: A study on "Which flavor of potato chips will be most popular among teenagers in Mumbai?" This research by Lay's is for the immediate purpose of deciding which product variant to launch.

3. CHARACTERISTICS OF A GOOD RESEARCH

  • Systematic and Logical: Research follows a structured, step-by-step approach.
  • Empirical and Objective: Based on observable evidence rather than opinions.
  • Valid and Reliable: Measures what it claims to measure (validity) and produces consistent results (reliability).
  • Generalizable: Results can be applied beyond the specific study sample to broader populations.
  • Relevant and Significant: Addresses important questions and provides meaningful insights.
  • Ethical: Protects participant privacy and follows ethical guidelines.

4. RESEARCH OBJECTIVES

Research objectives are specific, measurable goals that define what the research aims to achieve. They act as a roadmap, guiding every aspect of the research process.

Types of Research Objectives

A. Exploratory Objectives

To explore and understand a problem when little is known. For example, "To explore the potential opportunities and challenges for Starbucks entering tier-2 Indian cities."

B. Descriptive Objectives

To describe characteristics or phenomena in detail. For example, "To determine the size and growth rate of the Indian organic food market."

C. Causal Objectives

To establish cause-and-effect relationships between variables. For example, "To determine the effect of a price reduction on sales volume for Britannia biscuits."

5. TYPES OF RESEARCH

CLASSIFICATION BY PURPOSE

A. Exploratory Research

Used when the problem is not clearly defined. It is flexible, uses small samples, and qualitative methods to generate hypotheses. For example, Patanjali might use focus groups to explore entering the fitness equipment market.

B. Descriptive Research

Used to describe characteristics of markets or consumers. It is structured, uses large samples, and quantitative methods. For example, a large-scale survey to measure the size of the Indian smartphone market.

C. Causal Research

Used to understand cause-and-effect relationships. It is highly structured and uses experimental designs. For example, a field experiment in different cities to test the optimal price for a new Maggi variant.

CLASSIFICATION BY APPROACH

A. Quantitative Research

Focuses on numbers, statistics, and measurable data. Examples include customer satisfaction surveys and market size studies.

B. Qualitative Research

Focuses on understanding attitudes, motivations, and behaviors. Examples include focus groups and in-depth interviews.

C. Mixed Methods Research

Combines quantitative and qualitative approaches. For example, using qualitative focus groups to identify factors, then a quantitative survey to measure their importance.

7. RESEARCH PROPOSAL

A research proposal is a formal document that outlines a planned research project. It serves as a contract between researcher and client, detailing what will be done, how, when, and at what cost.

Types of Research Proposals

  • Internal Proposals: Created within a company for management. Example: Flipkart's research team proposing a customer retention study to senior management.
  • External Proposals: Written for an outside organization. Example: Nielsen proposing a retail measurement study to Hindustan Unilever.
  • Solicited Proposals: Submitted in response to a formal Request for Proposal (RFP). Example: Multiple agencies responding to a Tata Motors RFP for a customer satisfaction study.
  • Unsolicited Proposals: Initiated by a research agency without a specific client request. Example: An agency proactively approaching Zomato with a proposal for a voice ordering study.

8. RESEARCH PROPOSAL STRUCTURE

  1. Executive Summary: Concise overview of the entire proposal.
  2. Background and Problem Statement: Establishes context and justifies the need for research.
  3. Research Objectives: Clearly states what the research aims to achieve.
  4. Literature Review (if applicable): Demonstrates understanding of existing knowledge.
  5. Research Methodology: Detailed explanation of the research approach.
  6. Sample Design: Explains the target population and sampling approach.
  7. Data Collection Plan: Describes how data will be gathered.
  8. Data Analysis Plan: Explains how data will be analyzed.
  9. Timeline and Milestones: Shows the project schedule and key deliverables.
  10. Budget and Resources: Details project costs and resource requirements.
  11. Expected Outcomes and Deliverables: Describes what the client will receive.
  12. Research Team and Credentials: Establishes credibility and expertise.
  13. Terms and Conditions: Defines project parameters and responsibilities.

9. EVALUATING RESEARCH PROPOSALS

Evaluation Criteria

  • Technical Soundness: Appropriateness of methodology, sample design, and analysis plan.
  • Relevance and Alignment: Alignment with business objectives and actionability of insights.
  • Feasibility and Practicality: Realism of timeline and resource requirements.
  • Cost-Effectiveness: Value for money and budget transparency.
  • Credibility and Experience: Team expertise and past performance.

Common Proposal Weaknesses to Avoid

  • Vague Objectives
  • Methodology Mismatch
  • Inadequate Sampling
  • Unrealistic Timeline
  • Incomplete Budget
  • Generic Approach
