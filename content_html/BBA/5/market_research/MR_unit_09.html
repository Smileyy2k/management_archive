UNIT 9: ATTITUDE MEASUREMENT

This unit covers the essential techniques and scales used by market researchers to quantify people's attitudesâ€”their relatively enduring evaluations, feelings, and behavioral tendencies towards an object, person, or idea.

1. GENERAL METHODS OF COLLECTING ATTITUDE DATA

These are the broad approaches used to gather information about attitudes:

  • Self-Report (or Verbal Report): The most common method, where individuals are directly asked about their attitudes using surveys and questionnaires. It is direct and cost-effective but prone to social desirability bias.
  • Observation: Observing a person's behavior (e.g., purchasing patterns, website clicks) to infer their attitude. It measures actual behavior but doesn't explain the underlying motivation.
  • Physiological Measures (or Indirect Methods): Using biological responses (e.g., Galvanic Skin Response, pupil dilation) to gauge an emotional reaction. It provides objective data but can be expensive and ambiguous.

2. SPECIFIC METHODS OF COLLECTING ATTITUDINAL DATA

These refer to the structured tools, primarily scaling techniques, used to quantify attitudes. Scales convert subjective attitudes into measurable numerical scores for statistical analysis.

3. COMPARATIVE SCALES

Comparative scales require respondents to directly compare one stimulus object (e.g., a brand) against another. The resulting data is typically ordinal (ranks/order).

3.1 Paired Comparison

The respondent is presented with two objects at a time and asked to select one based on a criterion (e.g., "Which brand do you prefer?"). Best for a small number of brands.

3.2 Rank Order

The respondent is presented with several objects simultaneously and asked to rank them according to a specific criterion (e.g., 1st, 2nd, 3rd).

3.3 Constant Sum

Respondents allocate a fixed number of points (e.g., 100 points) among several characteristics based on importance. This forces trade-offs and provides ratio-level data.

3.4 Q-Sort

A scaling technique that uses a sophisticated rank order procedure to sort a large number of statements or objects into groups based on specified criteria, often forming a quasi-normal distribution.

4. NON-COMPARATIVE SCALES

Non-comparative scales (also called monadic scales) evaluate a single object independently. The resulting data is typically interval or ratio.

4.1 Likert Scale

Respondents indicate their degree of agreement or disagreement with a series of statements. The standard format is a 5-point scale (e.g., 1=Strongly Disagree to 5=Strongly Agree).

4.2 Semantic Differential

Respondents rate an object on a series of bipolar (opposite) adjectives or phrases, typically on a 7-point scale. Ideal for creating a "profile" of a brand.

Powerful --- 1 2 3 4 5 6 7 --- Weak

Modern --- 1 2 3 4 5 6 7 --- Old-fashioned

4.3 Stapel Scale

A unipolar (one-adjective) rating scale that measures both the direction and intensity of an attitude. It's typically a 10-point scale from +5 to -5, without a neutral zero.

+5 +4 +3 +2 +1 --- Excellent Customer Service --- -1 -2 -3 -4 -5

5. WHICH SCALE TO USE?

The choice of scale depends on the research objective, the level of detail required, and the planned statistical analysis.

Objective/Criterion Recommended Scale Type Reason
Simple Preference/Order Rank Order, Paired Comparison Quick, yields ordinal data.
Relative Importance Constant Sum Forces trade-offs, yields ratio data.
Intensity of Agreement Likert Scale Easy to construct and understand.
Brand/Product Profile Semantic Differential Visually represents the object's image.

6. MEASURING EMOTIONS

Measuring emotions goes beyond measuring beliefs and actions to focus on the affective (feeling) component of attitude. Key techniques include:

  • Self-Report: Using scales to describe feelings, such as the PAD (Pleasure-Arousal-Dominance) scale.
  • Physiological Measures (Neuromarketing):
    • Facial Coding: Analyzing micro-expressions to detect underlying emotions.
    • EEG/fMRI: Measuring brain activity to map emotional responses.
    • Eye Tracking: Measuring gaze duration and pupil dilation, which correlates with interest and arousal.

7. PERCEPTUAL MAPPING

Perceptual mapping is a visual technique used to display the perceptions of respondents about various brands on two or more dimensions. It's like a geographical map for brands: close brands are seen as similar, and distant brands as different. The axes are typically key attributes (e.g., Price vs. Quality).

Uses of perceptual mapping include identifying competition, detecting market gaps, and monitoring brand positioning.

For the Indian automobile industry, the two most critical dimensions are typically Affordability vs. Premium (X-Axis) and Reliability/Value vs. Safety/Innovation (Y-Axis).
