UNIT 11: SAMPLING AND DATA ANALYSIS

1. WHAT IS SAMPLING IN MARKET RESEARCH

Sampling is the systematic process of selecting a subset of elements from a larger group (the population) with the expectation that the information gathered from the subset will allow for accurate conclusions about the entire group. Since we can't talk to every potential customer, we select a manageable, representative group (the sample). If this sample is chosen scientifically, their opinions act as a reliable proxy for the opinions of the entire market.

2. SAMPLING TERMINOLOGY

  • Population (or Universe): The entire group of people or objects of interest (e.g., all potential buyers of electric cars in a city).
  • Element: The unit about which information is sought (e.g., a single electric car buyer).
  • Sampling Unit: An element, or a group of elements, available for selection (e.g., a specific household).
  • Sampling Frame: A physical list or database of all the sampling units from which the sample is drawn (e.g., a list of all motor vehicle registrations).
  • Sample: The actual subset of elements drawn from the sampling frame that participates in the study.
  • Census: A data collection process that includes every single element in the population.

3. NEED FOR SAMPLING

Sampling is essential in market research for several practical reasons:

  1. Cost Savings: Surveying a sample is significantly cheaper than a census.
  2. Time Savings: Data collection is much faster for a small sample.
  3. Feasibility: The population may be infinite or geographically dispersed, making a census impossible.
  4. Accuracy: A well-executed sample can sometimes provide more accurate results than a poorly executed census because resources are concentrated on reducing non-sampling error.

4. CHARACTERISTICS OF A GOOD SAMPLE

A good sample ensures the results are reliable and projectable.

  • Representativeness: The sample must accurately reflect the characteristics of the population (e.g., proportional balance of age, gender).
  • Accuracy: The sample results closely approximate the results that would have been obtained from a census.
  • Precision: The results exhibit minimal random sampling error. Precision is increased by increasing the sample size.

5. SAMPLING DESIGNS

Sampling Design refers to the master plan for selecting the sample elements. The main choice is between two categories:

Type Key Feature Explanation
Probability Sampling Every element in the population has a known, non-zero chance of being selected. Statistical Generalization. This is the scientific standard. It allows us to calculate sampling error and project results to the entire population.
Non-Probability Sampling The selection of elements is based on the researcher's subjective judgment or convenience. Quick Insights. Useful for exploratory research. We cannot generalize results or measure sampling error.

6. SAMPLING CONCEPTS

A. Parameter vs. Statistic

  • Parameter: A numerical characteristic of the population (e.g., the average age of all customers). It is usually unknown.
  • Statistic: A numerical characteristic of the sample (e.g., the average age of the 500 sampled customers). It is used to estimate the parameter.

B. Sampling Error

The difference between the sample statistic and the population parameter, caused by the sample not being a perfect representation. It's the natural, unavoidable mismatch that happens because you're only looking at a subset. It is reduced by increasing the sample size.

C. Non-Sampling Error (Systematic Error)

Errors that arise from sources other than the sampling procedure, such as interviewer bias, measurement error, or flaws in questionnaire design. These are human or design mistakes and cannot be reduced by simply increasing the sample size.

7. CALCULATION OF SAMPLE SIZE

The calculation of sample size (n) for estimating population characteristics relies on balancing three factors: Precision, Confidence, and Variability.

  • Level of Precision (E): The desired margin of error (e.g., ±5%).
  • Level of Confidence (Z): The probability that the confidence interval will contain the true population parameter (95% confidence uses Z=1.96).
  • Degree of Variability (σ or π): The heterogeneity or variance of the population. Higher variability requires a larger sample.

Formula for Sample Size (Estimating Mean):

$n = Z^2\sigma^2 / E^2$

  • n: Required Sample Size
  • Z: Z-score for the confidence level
  • σ: Population standard deviation (must be estimated)
  • E: Maximum allowable error (Precision)

8. TYPES OF SAMPLE DESIGNS

A. Non-Probability Sampling Methods (Bias Risk is High)

  1. Convenience Sampling: Selection of units that are easily accessible. Use: Exploratory research.
  2. Judgmental Sampling: Researcher uses expert judgment to select members. Use: Qualitative research.
  3. Quota Sampling: Ensures the sample matches the population on pre-specified characteristics (e.g., gender, age), but selection within quotas is non-random. Use: Common when time and budget are limited.
  4. Snowball Sampling: Initial respondents refer other potential respondents. Use: Finding rare or specialized populations.

B. Probability Sampling Methods (Allows Statistical Inference)

  1. Simple Random Sampling (SRS): Each element has an equal and known probability of being selected. High validity, but costly and requires a perfect list of the population.
  2. Systematic Sampling: Sample is chosen by selecting a random start and then picking every kth element. Simpler than SRS, but risky if there is a hidden pattern in the list.
  3. Stratified Sampling: Population is divided into homogeneous subgroups (strata), and a random sample is drawn from each. Ensures adequate representation of all subgroups.
  4. Cluster Sampling: Population is divided into subgroups (clusters), and a random sample of clusters is selected. All elements in selected clusters are surveyed. Cost-effective, but less precise than SRS.
