UNIT 1: Marketing Management: The Development of a Concept

1. DEFINITION OF MARKETING MANAGEMENT

What is Marketing Management?

Marketing Management is the art and science of choosing target markets and getting, keeping, and growing customers through creating, delivering, and communicating superior customer value. The American Marketing Association (AMA) defines marketing as, "The process of planning and executing the conception, pricing, promotion and distribution of ideas, goods and services to create exchanges that satisfy individual and organizational objectives."

Key Components:

  • Planning: Deciding what products to offer, which customers to target, and how to reach them.
  • Organizing: Arranging resources (people, money, technology) to execute marketing plans.
  • Implementing: Putting marketing strategies into action through day-to-day activities.
  • Controlling: Monitoring results and making adjustments to improve performance.

The Concept of Exchange

Exchange is the core concept of marketing. It occurs when two or more parties give something of value to each other to satisfy their respective needs.

Conditions for Exchange:
  1. At least two parties must exist.
  2. Each party has something of value to the other.
  3. Each party is capable of communication and delivery.
  4. Each party is free to accept or reject the exchange.
  5. Each party believes it's appropriate to deal with the other.

Needs vs. Wants

Needs: Basic human requirements for survival and well-being (e.g., food, water, shelter, social belonging).

Wants: Specific forms that needs take based on culture, personality, and individual preferences (e.g., Need: Thirst; Want: Coca-Cola).

Economic Utility

Economic Utility refers to the satisfaction or benefit that consumers derive from consuming a product or service. Marketing creates four types of utility:

  • Form Utility: Creating products in the form customers want (e.g., Amul converting milk into butter and cheese).
  • Place Utility: Making products available where customers want them (e.g., Flipkart delivering to doorsteps).
  • Time Utility: Making products available when customers want them (e.g., 24/7 pharmacies).
  • Possession Utility: Facilitating the transfer of ownership (e.g., Easy EMI options).

2. EVOLUTION OF MARKETING

Marketing has evolved through distinct eras, each with a different business philosophy.

  1. Production Era (1860s-1920s): Philosophy: "Produce what you can sell." Focus on manufacturing efficiency as demand exceeded supply. Little concern for customer preferences.
  2. Sales Era (1920s-1950s): Philosophy: "Sell what you produce." Emphasis on aggressive selling and promotion as supply began to exceed demand.
  3. Marketing Era (1950s-Present): Philosophy: "Produce what customers want." A customer-centric approach focused on market research, segmentation, and building long-term relationships.

3. MARKETING CONCEPT

The Marketing Concept is a business philosophy that holds that achieving organizational goals depends on knowing the needs and wants of target markets and delivering satisfaction better than competitors. Its three core features are:

  • Consumer Orientation: Placing the customer at the center of all business decisions, from product development to service.
  • Long-Term Profitability: Focusing on sustainable profits through customer satisfaction and loyalty rather than short-term gains.
  • Functional Integration: Coordinating all business functions (production, finance, HR) to serve customer needs effectively.

4. SOCIETAL MARKETING CONCEPT

The Societal Marketing Concept holds that organizations should deliver superior value in a way that maintains or improves both the consumer's and society's well-being. It balances three key principles: Consumer Satisfaction, Company Profits, and Society's Welfare.

Example: Tata Motors' Electric Vehicle Initiative. It provides eco-friendly transportation (consumer satisfaction), captures the growing EV market (company profits), and reduces air pollution (social welfare).

5. MARKETING MYOPIA

Marketing Myopia, a concept by Theodore Levitt, refers to the short-sighted approach of businesses that focus too narrowly on their products rather than on customer needs and market opportunities.

How to Avoid Marketing Myopia:

  1. Define the Business in Terms of Customer Needs, Not Products: A railroad company should see itself in the "transportation business," not the "railroad business."
  2. Focus on the Customer, Not the Product: Put the customer at the center of all business thinking.
  3. Take a Long-Term View of Business Growth: Anticipate future changes and invest in R&D.
  4. Encourage Innovation and Experimentation: Create a culture that welcomes new ideas and learns from failure.

6. SIGNIFICANCE OF MARKETING IN DIFFERENT INDUSTRIES

6.1 Airline Industry

Marketing is crucial due to high fixed costs, perishable inventory (empty seats), and service intangibility. Key strategies include:

  • Frequent Flyer Programs: Build customer loyalty (e.g., IndiGo's 6E Rewards).
  • Strategic Partnerships: Expand network reach through code-sharing (e.g., Vistara-Singapore Airlines).
  • Ancillary Revenue Strategies: Generate income beyond ticket sales (e.g., baggage fees, in-flight meals).
  • Digital Marketing: Improve customer experience through mobile apps and social media engagement.

6.2 Banking Industry

Marketing in this highly regulated, trust-based industry focuses on relationships and technology. Key strategies include:

  • Digital Banking Initiatives: Meet convenience expectations (e.g., HDFC's PayZapp, SBI's YONO app).
  • Financial Inclusion Programs: Expand customer base in underserved markets (e.g., Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana).
  • Customer Experience Enhancement: Streamline processes (e.g., Kotak's 811 digital account opening).
  • Strategic Partnerships: Leverage external ecosystems (e.g., Amazon Pay-ICICI Bank Credit Card).

6.3 Education Industry

Marketing an intangible, high-involvement service requires a focus on reputation and outcomes. Key strategies include:

  • Digital Marketing: Reach tech-savvy students (e.g., BYJU'S video content, Unacademy's influencer marketing).
  • Industry Partnerships: Demonstrate employment outcomes (e.g., Amity's tie-ups with Microsoft and IBM).
  • Alumni Network Leveraging: Use success stories for credibility (e.g., IITs highlighting alumni like Sundar Pichai).
  • Scholarships and Financial Aid: Make education accessible and attract talent.
