UNIT 4: STRATEGIC PLANNING PROCESS IN MARKETING

1. DEFINING STRATEGIC MARKET PLANNING

Strategic market planning is the process of creating a long-term roadmap for a business to achieve its marketing goals by aligning them with its overall business objectives. It involves analyzing the market and competitive landscape to make informed decisions about the marketing mix.

Importance of Strategic Market Planning:

  • Provides a Clear Direction: Acts as a roadmap, ensuring all marketing activities are aligned with the company's vision.
  • Enhances Competitive Advantage: By analyzing competitors, a firm can identify its unique strengths and create a powerful value proposition.
  • Optimizes Resource Allocation: Forces a company to prioritize efforts and allocate resources to the most impactful activities.
  • Promotes Proactive Decision-Making: Allows a company to anticipate future trends and challenges rather than just reacting to them.

2. CORPORATE AND DIVISIONAL STRATEGIC PLANNING

This multi-level process guides a company's growth and resource allocation, starting from a broad corporate vision.

  1. Corporate Mission: Defines the company's purpose, values, and direction.
  2. Establishment of SBUs (Strategic Business Units): A large company is broken down into units that operate like separate businesses, each with its own mission and competitors.
  3. Resource Allocation to SBUs: Corporate headquarters decides how to allocate resources among SBUs, often using tools like the BCG Matrix or the GE 9-Cell Grid.

3.1 BCG Competitive Advantage Matrix

The Boston Consulting Group (BCG) matrix classifies SBUs based on market growth rate and relative market share into four categories:

  • Stars: High market share, high growth rate. Require significant investment.
  • Cash Cows: High market share, low growth rate. Mature, profitable businesses that generate surplus cash.
  • Question Marks: Low market share, high growth rate. New ventures with uncertain futures that require heavy investment.
  • Dogs: Low market share, low growth rate. Often generate only enough cash to maintain themselves and may be divested.

Planning New Business and Downsizing

Companies must have strategies for both growth and managing underperforming units.

  • Intensive Growth: Growing the core business through market penetration, market development, or product development.
  • Integrative Growth: Acquiring or merging with other businesses in the industry (backward, forward, or horizontal integration).
  • Diversification Growth: Pursuing opportunities outside the current business lines.
  • Downsizing Older Businesses: Strategically eliminating or reducing a business unit that is no longer profitable through divestment, harvesting, or liquidation.

3. STRATEGIC BUSINESS PLANNING

This is a structured process to define and achieve a company's long-term goals.

  1. Business Mission: Defining the company's purpose and what value it provides.
  2. SWOT Analysis: A framework for evaluating the company's internal Strengths and Weaknesses, and external Opportunities and Threats.
  3. Strategies: Formulating corporate-level, business-level, and functional-level strategies to achieve the mission.
  4. Strategic Alliances: Forming collaborative partnerships to achieve mutually beneficial goals.
  5. Implementation: Putting the strategic plan into action by allocating resources and assigning responsibilities.
  6. Feedback and Control: Monitoring the plan's execution, measuring results with KPIs, and making corrective actions.

4. MARKETING PROCESS

The marketing process is a series of steps to understand customer needs, create and deliver value, and build strong relationships.

1. Value Delivery Sequence

A modern approach emphasizing a superior customer experience, consisting of three phases:

  • Choosing the Value: The strategic phase of market research, segmentation, targeting, and positioning.
  • Providing the Value: The physical creation and delivery of the product, including features, pricing, and distribution.
  • Communicating the Value: Marketing communications to inform and persuade the target market, including advertising and promotions.

2. Steps in the Planning Process

  1. Analyzing Marketing Opportunities: Scanning the environment and using SWOT analysis to identify potential markets and customer needs.
  2. Developing Marketing Strategies: Designing a customer-driven strategy based on market segmentation, targeting, and positioning.
  3. Planning Marketing Programs: Translating the strategy into a detailed action plan using the marketing mix (the 4 Ps: Product, Price, Place, Promotion).
  4. Managing the Marketing Effort: Implementing the plan, controlling and measuring results, and gathering feedback for continuous improvement.

5. MARKETING PLAN

A marketing plan is a detailed, documented roadmap that outlines the marketing activities of a business. A typical plan includes:

  1. Executive Summary: A concise overview of the plan's main goals and strategies.
  2. Opportunity and Issue Analysis: An in-depth look at the current situation, including a SWOT analysis.
  3. Marketing Strategies: The strategic approach to achieve marketing objectives, including the target market and positioning.
  4. Action Programs (The Marketing Mix): Concrete, actionable steps detailing the implementation of the 4 Ps.
  5. Projected Profit and Loss Statement: A financial forecast of the plan's expected results.
  6. Controls: An explanation of how the plan's progress will be monitored using KPIs and adjusted as needed.
