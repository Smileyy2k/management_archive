UNIT 11: MARKETING OF SERVICES

1. IMPORTANCE OF SERVICES IN MARKETING

Services are activities, benefits, or satisfactions offered for sale that are essentially intangible and do not result in the ownership of anything. Services constitute the majority of economic activity in developed nations.

Significance: Services drive modern economies, contribute significantly to GDP, and are a source of competitive advantage.

Example: Education is a crucial economic sector. Universities provide the service of knowledge creation, skill development, and career advancement, fundamentally transforming the "customer" (student).

2. BASES FOR SERVICE CLASSIFICATION

2.1 Classification on the basis of the degree of involvement of the customer

This classifies services based on what is being processed or acted upon.

  • People Processing: Tangible actions on the customer's body (e.g., haircut).
  • Possession Processing: Tangible actions on the customer's physical goods (e.g., car repair).
  • Mental Stimulus Processing: Actions directed at the customer's mind (e.g., entertainment, education).
  • Information Processing: Actions directed at intangible assets (e.g., banking, insurance).

2.2 Classification on the basis of the service tangibility

  • Highly Intangible: The service is almost entirely an experience (e.g., consulting, education).
  • Service linked to Tangible Goods: Maintenance contracts for machinery.
  • Tangible Goods linked to Services: Restaurant food, a packaged travel tour.

2.3 Classification on the basis of skills and expertise required

  • Professional Services: Services requiring specialized knowledge and training (e.g., healthcare, law, teaching).
  • Non-Professional Services: Services requiring less specialized skills (e.g., cleaning).

2.4 Classification on the basis of the business orientation of service provider

  • Commercial Organizations: Primary goal is profit.
  • Not-for-Profit Organizations: Primary goal is social welfare, education, or mission.

2.5 Classification on the basis of the type of end-user

  • Consumer Services: Used by individuals for personal needs (e.g., healthcare).
  • B2B Services: Services sold to other organizations (e.g., corporate insurance).

3. CHARACTERISTICS OF SERVICES (IHIP)

Services possess four fundamental characteristics that distinguish them from physical goods.

  • Intangibility: Services cannot be seen, tasted, felt, heard, or smelled before they are bought.
  • Heterogeneity (Variability): The quality of services depends on who provides them, when, where, and how. It is difficult to standardize output.
  • Inseparability: Services are typically produced and consumed at the same time.
  • Perishability: Services cannot be stored for later sale or use.

4. DEVELOPING MARKETING STRATEGIES FOR SERVICES (THE 7 PS)

The traditional 4 Ps are expanded to 7 Ps to account for the unique characteristics of services.

  1. Product: The service offering, features, and quality.
  2. Price: Fees, discounts, and payment terms.
  3. Promotion: Communicating the value of the service.
  4. Place/Distribution: The channel used to deliver the service.
  5. People: All human actors who play a part in service delivery (employees, the customer, other customers).
  6. Process: The procedures, mechanisms, and flow of activities by which the service is delivered.
  7. Physical Evidence: The environment in which the service is delivered and any tangible elements that facilitate it.

5. MANAGING SERVICE DIFFERENTIATION

To stand out, a service firm must differentiate itself based on:

  • Offer (What is offered): Creating a unique, specialized curriculum that no competitor offers.
  • Delivery (How it is offered): Ensuring tiny class sizes with a guaranteed low student-to-faculty ratio.
  • Image (How it is perceived): Promoting a top global ranking for job placement in a key industry.

6. MANAGING SERVICE QUALITY

High service quality is critical for building loyalty. This involves:

  • Strategic Concept: Embedding quality in the company's mission.
  • Commitment of the Top Management: Quality must be driven by senior leadership.
  • High Standards: Establishing measurable standards for service performance.
  • Monitoring Systems: Regularly measuring customer satisfaction and service performance.
  • Customer Complaints: Having effective systems to resolve complaints quickly and fairly.
  • Satisfying Employees: High-quality service requires motivated, well-trained employees.

7. MANAGING PRODUCTIVITY

Finding ways to improve efficiency without sacrificing the perceived quality of the service.

Example: Using AI-driven tutoring software for routine queries to free up professors' time for complex mentorship, thus gaining efficiency while maintaining quality.

8. PRODUCT SUPPORT SERVICE MANAGEMENT

Services provided to support the primary product or service, often extending beyond the core transaction.

Example: A university providing lifelong career guidance to alumni, offering exclusive networking events, and allowing graduates to audit new courses for free years after graduation.
