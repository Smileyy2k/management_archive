UNIT 3: MARKETING ENVIRONMENT

The performance of a company depends on its marketing environment, which influences its marketing activities and, ultimately, its sales. The demand for a product is affected by the environment in which the organization functions, as this environment shapes customer needs and wants.

1. COMPETITIVE FORCES

Competition has a significant impact on the marketing environment. The level of competition depends on the structure of an industry.

Types of Competitive Structures

  • Pure Competition: A market with a large number of firms selling identical products. No single firm can influence the market price; every firm is a price taker (e.g., agricultural products like wheat).
  • Monopolistic Competition: Features many firms selling differentiated products. Firms have some limited control over their prices and compete heavily on branding and advertising (e.g., restaurants, clothing stores).
  • Oligopoly: A market dominated by a small number of large firms that are highly interdependent. The actions of one firm directly affect the others (e.g., airline and automobile industries).
  • Monopoly: A market with a single seller who has complete control over the market for a particular good with no close substitutes (e.g., utility companies providing water or electricity).

2. MACRO ENVIRONMENTAL FACTORS

Macro-environmental factors are large-scale, external forces that affect a business's operations. They are often analyzed using frameworks like PESTLE (Political, Economic, Socio-cultural, Technological, Legal, Environmental).

  • Demographic Environment: The study of human populations in terms of size, density, age, gender, occupation, etc. Changes in these characteristics can create new markets.
  • Political Environment: Government policies, laws, and political stability that regulate a business's operations.
  • Economic Environment: Broad economic conditions like GDP trends, inflation, and unemployment that influence consumer spending and business costs.
  • Socio-Cultural Environment: Societal forces that affect a population's basic values, preferences, and behaviors (e.g., the increasing focus on health and wellness).
  • Technology Environment: Advancements in technology that create new products and market opportunities, and can disrupt existing industries.
  • Natural Environment: Physical resources and conditions like raw materials availability and climate change that affect businesses.
  • Legal Environment: Laws, government agencies, and pressure groups that influence and restrict organizations, including consumer protection laws.

3. PORTER'S FIVE FORCE MODEL

Porter's Five Forces Model is a framework for analyzing the competitive intensity and profitability of an industry. It identifies five key forces that shape competition.

  1. Threat of New Entrants: This assesses how easy or difficult it is for new companies to enter an industry. High barriers to entry (e.g., high capital requirements, strong brand loyalty) protect existing firms.
  2. Bargaining Power of Suppliers: This measures the power suppliers have to increase prices. A supplier group is powerful if it is concentrated or its product is unique.
  3. Bargaining Power of Buyers: This assesses the power customers have to drive down prices. Buyers have more power when they purchase large volumes or have many substitute options.
  4. Threat of Substitute Products or Services: A substitute is a different product that can satisfy the same customer need. The threat is high if the substitute offers a good price-performance trade-off.
  5. Rivalry Among Existing Competitors: This central force evaluates the intensity of competition between firms already in the industry. Rivalry is fierce when there are many competitors of similar size and slow industry growth.
