UNIT 8: PRODUCT DIFFERENTIATION AND POSITIONING

Differentiation is the act of making a product or service unique, while positioning is the act of creating a distinct and desirable image for that product in the minds of the target audience.

1. PRODUCT DIFFERENTIATION

This is the process of creating a product with unique attributes to distinguish it from competitors. Differentiation can be based on:

  • Product Form: The size, shape, or physical structure (e.g., the iPhone's sleek design at its launch).
  • Design: The totality of features affecting how a product looks and functions (e.g., Apple's elegant and intuitive user experience).
  • Features: Characteristics that provide a specific benefit (e.g., a smartphone with a pop-up camera).
  • Size of Package: Offering unique or convenient package sizes.
  • Product Quality: The level of performance, reliability, and durability (e.g., Rolex's craftsmanship).

2. SERVICE DIFFERENTIATION

This is about providing a unique and valuable service that goes beyond the core product. Examples include:

  • Ordering Ease: How simple it is to place an order (e.g., Domino's "zero-click" ordering app).
  • Delivery: The speed and accuracy of delivery (e.g., Amazon Prime's same-day delivery).
  • Installation: Services to set up the product.
  • Guarantees: A strong warranty that reduces customer risk.
  • Customer Training: Providing training on how to use the product effectively.
  • Maintenance and Repair: The quality and speed of repair services.

3. PERSONNEL DIFFERENTIATION

Gaining a competitive advantage by having better trained, more competent, and more effective people than competitors. This is especially crucial in the service industry.

  • Competence: The employee's skill and knowledge.
  • Courteousness: The politeness, respect, and friendliness of employees.
  • Credibility: The extent to which employees are trustworthy and believable.
  • Reliability: The ability to perform the promised service accurately and dependably.
  • Responsiveness: The willingness to help customers and provide prompt service.

4. CHANNEL DIFFERENTIATION

Gaining an advantage by designing a unique and superior distribution channel. This involves the entire journey from manufacturer to consumer.

  • Direct-to-Consumer (D2C) Model: Selling directly to consumers, bypassing traditional retailers (e.g., Dell selling customized computers online).
  • Exclusive Distribution: Giving a single reseller the exclusive right to sell products in a specific area (e.g., luxury brands like Louis Vuitton).
  • Intensive Distribution: Putting the product in as many outlets as possible (e.g., Coca-Cola).

5. IMAGE DIFFERENTIATION

Creating a powerful and lasting brand image in the minds of customers. This can be achieved through:

  • Symbols: A specific symbol to create a distinct image (e.g., the Nike "swoosh").
  • Logos: A visual representation of the brand (e.g., the Coca-Cola or Apple logos).
  • Atmosphere: The physical environment where the product is sold (e.g., the minimalist, high-tech feel of an Apple Store).
  • Events: Sponsoring events to build a brand image (e.g., Red Bull sponsoring extreme sports).

6. POSITIONING

Positioning is the act of creating a distinct and desirable place in the mind of the target consumer. It's about what a brand stands for relative to competitors.

  • Getting into the mind of the customer: The goal is to create a clear association between the brand and a specific benefit (e.g., Volvo is synonymous with safety).
  • Positioning of a follower: A market follower positions itself as a strong alternative to the leader (e.g., Pepsi positioned as "The Choice of a New Generation" to challenge Coca-Cola).
  • Repositioning the competition: A direct attack to change how customers perceive a rival brand (e.g., Apple's "Get a Mac" ad campaign contrasting Mac's cool image with the PC's boring one).

Pitfalls in Positioning

Marketers must avoid common mistakes:

  • Under-positioning: Not communicating a clear benefit, resulting in a vague image.
  • Over-positioning: Giving the brand a too-narrow image.
  • Confused positioning: Communicating too many benefits or changing positioning too often.
  • Doubtful positioning: Making a claim that customers find hard to believe.
