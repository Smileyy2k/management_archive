UNIT 7: MARKET SEGMENTATION AND MARKET TARGETING

1. MARKET SEGMENTATION AND MARKET TARGETING

Market segmentation is the process of dividing a broad market into sub-groups of consumers (segments) based on shared characteristics. Market targeting then involves evaluating each segment's attractiveness and selecting one or more segments to serve. This allows companies to focus their resources and develop tailored marketing mixes.

2. NEED FOR SEGMENTING MARKETS

Segmentation is essential for several key reasons:

  • Consumer Heterogeneity: Consumers' needs and preferences vary widely. A single product cannot satisfy everyone.
  • Resource Optimization: Companies have limited resources. Segmentation allows them to focus their marketing budget on groups most likely to respond, maximizing ROI.
  • Competitive Advantage: By serving a specific segment (a niche), a company can create a strong, defensible position in the market.
  • Improved Customer Understanding: The process forces companies to deeply analyze their customers, leading to products that precisely meet their needs and build loyalty.

3. MARKET SEGMENTATION LEVELS

Markets can be segmented at different levels of specificity:

  • Segment Marketing: A company identifies a large, identifiable group with similar needs (e.g., an airline offering business class for travelers valuing comfort and economy class for those prioritizing affordability).
  • Individual Marketing: Customizing products and marketing for a single customer, also known as "one-to-one marketing" (e.g., Netflix recommending content based on viewing history).
  • Niche Marketing: Focusing on a more narrowly defined group seeking a distinct set of benefits (e.g., a company selling only specialized rock climbing equipment).
  • Local Marketing: Tailoring marketing to the needs of local customer groups in specific geographical areas (e.g., a retail chain selling beachwear in coastal cities).

4. SELECTION OF SEGMENTATION VARIABLES

4.1 Criteria for Segmenting Consumer Market

Consumer markets can be segmented using a wide range of variables:

  • Geographic: Dividing the market into units like nations, states, or cities.
  • Demographic: Based on variables like age, gender, income, occupation, and social class.
  • Psychographic: Based on lifestyle, personality, and values (e.g., targeting consumers with an active, health-conscious lifestyle).
  • Behavioral: Based on their knowledge of, attitude toward, use of, or response to a product. This includes:
    • Occasions: Marketing for specific occasions like Valentine's Day.
    • Benefits: Targeting specific benefits like whitening or cavity-fighting toothpaste.
    • Usage Rate: Different tiers for heavy, medium, and light users.
    • Loyalty Status: Hard-core loyals, split loyals, shifting loyals, and switchers.

4.2 Criteria for Segmenting Organizational Markets

Organizational markets are segmented using business-specific variables:

  • Demographics: Industry, company size, and customer location.
  • Operating Variables: Company technology, product use status, and customer capabilities.
  • Purchasing Approaches: Organization's purchasing function, power structures, and general purchasing policies.
  • Situational Factors: Urgency of order, product application, and size of the order.
  • Personal Characteristics: The individual preferences and relationships of the buyers.

5. EFFECTIVE SEGMENTATION

For a market segment to be useful, it must be:

  • Measurable: The size, purchasing power, and characteristics must be quantifiable.
  • Substantial: The segment must be large and profitable enough to serve.
  • Accessible: The segment must be effectively reachable with marketing efforts.
  • Differentiable: Segments must be conceptually distinguishable and respond differently to marketing mixes.
  • Actionable: The company must have the resources to design and implement effective programs for the segment.

6. TARGET MARKET SELECTION PROCESS

6.1 Evaluating the Market Segments

A company evaluates segments based on two main factors:

  • Segment Attractiveness: The size, growth, profitability, and low risk of a segment.
  • Company Objectives and Resources: The segment must align with the company's long-term goals and capabilities.

6.2 Selecting the Market Segments

A company can choose from five patterns for selection:

  1. Single Segment Concentration: The company focuses on a single segment, becoming a specialist.
  2. Selective Specialization: The company selects a number of attractive segments that fit its objectives.
  3. Product Specialization: The company focuses on making a specific product and sells it to several different segments.
  4. Market Specialization: The company focuses on serving the needs of a particular customer group with a variety of products.
  5. Full Market Coverage: The company attempts to serve all customer groups with all the products they might need.
