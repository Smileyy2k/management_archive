UNIT 6: ORGANIZATIONAL MARKETS AND ORGANIZATIONAL BUYING BEHAVIOR

1. THE CONCEPT OF ORGANIZATIONAL BUYING

Organizational buying, or business-to-business (B2B) buying, is the process of purchasing goods and services for use in producing other goods, for resale, or for daily operations.

Differences between Organizational and Consumer Markets

Organizational markets have fewer but larger buyers, longer and more complex buying cycles, and purchases are made in bulk quantities. Consumer markets have many individual buyers, shorter buying cycles, and small-quantity purchases.

Types of Organizational Buying Situations

  • New Task: The most complex situation where a product is purchased for the first time, requiring extensive information search.
  • Straight Rebuy: A routine repurchase of an item the company has bought before, with little to no information search.
  • Modified Rebuy: A situation where the buyer wants to modify product specifications, prices, or suppliers, requiring some information search.

System Buying and Selling

This involves purchasing a complete solution to a problem from a single seller, rather than buying individual components from different sellers (e.g., buying a "turnkey" data center solution from one provider).

2. DIMENSIONS OF ORGANIZATIONAL BUYING

Organizational buying is a complex process shaped by several interconnected dimensions:

  • Decision-Making Process: A structured sequence guided by inventory and logistics strategies like Material Requirements Planning (MRP), Just-in-Time (JIT), or Just-in-Case (JIC).
  • Centralization of Purchasing Process: A strategic dimension that dictates where buying decisions are made. Centralized purchasing allows for bulk buying and standardization.
  • Reverse Marketing: When a buyer actively seeks out a supplier for a specific need that current suppliers can't meet.
  • Sample Checking: A quality control dimension involving testing a small portion of a delivered batch to ensure quality standards.
  • Vendor and Supplier Analysis: A continuous process of evaluating and managing supplier performance based on criteria like cost, quality, and reliability.

3. DIMENSIONS OF ORGANIZATIONAL DEMAND

Organizational demand is fundamentally different from consumer demand and has several key characteristics:

  • Inelastic Demand: The demand for a business's products is not significantly affected by a change in their price, as the cost of one component is often a small fraction of the final product's total cost.
  • Derived Demand: The most significant characteristic. Demand for organizational goods is directly derived from the demand for consumer goods.
  • Joint Demand: Occurs when the demand for one product is directly linked to the demand for another (e.g., the demand for tires is joint with the demand for wheels).
  • Fluctuating Demand: Demand can be highly volatile. A small change in consumer demand can lead to a much larger change in organizational demand (the accelerator effect).

4. CLASSIFICATION OF ORGANIZATIONAL MARKETS

Organizational markets are classified based on the purpose for which buyers purchase goods and services:

  1. Producer Markets: Organizations that buy products to use in the production of other goods (e.g., a car manufacturer buying steel).
  2. Reseller Markets: Intermediaries like wholesalers and retailers that buy finished goods and resell them for a profit (e.g., a supermarket buying packaged food).
  3. Government Markets: Federal, state, and local government units that purchase goods and services to carry out their functions (e.g., the Ministry of Defense buying military equipment).
  4. Institutional Markets: Non-profit organizations like schools, hospitals, and charities that buy goods to support their operations.

5. PARTICIPANTS IN ORGANIZATIONAL BUYING (THE BUYING CENTER)

In organizational buying, multiple individuals, known as the buying center, participate in the decision. Each member plays a distinct role:

  • Initiators: First recognize a problem or need.
  • Users: Will actually use the product or service.
  • Influencers: Affect the buying decision by providing advice and criteria.
  • Deciders: Have the authority to make the final choice.
  • Approvers: Authorize the proposed actions of deciders.
  • Buyers: Have the formal authority to select the supplier and arrange purchase terms.
  • Gatekeepers: Control the flow of information to other members of the buying center.

6. STAGES OF BUYING

The organizational buying process is a structured sequence of stages:

  1. Problem Recognition: Recognizing a problem or need that can be met by acquiring a good or service.
  2. General Need Recognition: Defining the general characteristics and quantity of the needed item.
  3. Product Specification: Developing a detailed technical specification for the product.
  4. Searching for Potential Suppliers: Searching for qualified suppliers who can meet the specifications.
  5. Value Analysis: A systematic approach to cost reduction by studying product components.
  6. Vendor Analysis: Evaluating potential suppliers based on criteria like price, quality, and service.
  7. Order Routine Specification: Preparing the final order with exact quantity, specifications, and terms.
  8. Performance Review: Reviewing the performance of the chosen supplier to influence future decisions.
