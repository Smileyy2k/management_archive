UNIT 10: GLOBAL MARKETING STRATEGIES

1. SIGNIFICANCE OF GLOBAL MARKETING

Global Marketing refers to coordinating marketing activities across multiple countries. It involves identifying global market segments, positioning, and determining marketing mix strategies (4 Ps) that are either consistent globally or adapted where necessary.

Significance:

  • Market Growth: Global markets offer significant opportunities when domestic markets are saturated.
  • Competitive Advantage: Operating globally allows firms to achieve economies of scale and leverage resources.
  • Survival: Companies may need to go international to follow their customers or competitors.
  • Product Life Cycle Extension: Mature domestic products may find renewed growth in less-developed foreign markets.

2. SELECTING A POTENTIAL MARKET

The process of selecting which foreign markets to enter involves systematic screening and evaluation.

  1. Phase 1: Analyze Macro Environmental Factors: Initial screening based on broad factors like geography, demographics, economic indicators, and political stability.
  2. Phase 2: Analyze Market Size, Product Acceptability, and Customer Perceptions: A closer look at specific market attractiveness, including consumption levels, cultural fit, and brand perception.
  3. Phase 3: Analyze Micro Environmental Factors: Detailed scrutiny of operational aspects like distribution channels, local competition (using Porter's Five Forces), and cost of entry.
  4. Phase 4: Ranking Potential Markets: Markets are ranked based on potential profit, risk, and strategic fit to decide on the sequence and scale of entry.

3. IMPACT OF ENVIRONMENTAL FORCES ON GLOBAL MARKETING

Global marketers must adapt their strategies to various environmental forces:

  • Economic Environment: Factors like income levels, purchasing power, and currency exchange rates.
  • Political Environment: Government stability, trade regulations (tariffs, quotas), and investment policies.
  • Social and Cultural Environment: Encompasses language, values, beliefs, aesthetics, and customs. This is often the most difficult force to manage.
  • Legal and Regulatory Environment: Differences in laws governing product safety, advertising, and intellectual property.
  • Technological Environment: The level of technological development dictates how products are consumed, advertised, and distributed.

4. METHODS OF ENTERING A NEW MARKET

Market entry modes represent a continuum of commitment, risk, control, and profit potential.

  • Indirect Exports: Selling through domestic intermediaries. Lowest risk and investment, but also low control and profit.
  • Direct Exports: Selling directly to foreign buyers. Higher control and profit, but also higher costs.
  • Licensing: Granting a foreign company the right to use intellectual property for a fee. Low risk and quick entry, but with loss of control.
  • Joint Ventures: Sharing ownership and control of a new local business with a local partner. Access to local knowledge and shared risks, but potential for conflict.

5. TYPES OF MARKETING ORGANIZATION

As a firm's international commitment grows, its organizational structure must evolve.

  • Export Division: The initial structure, where a domestically-based staff focuses on export duties.
  • International Division: A separate division is created to manage all international activities as global sales increase.
  • Global Organization: For companies where global operations are critical, organizing around global product groups, geographic regions, or a complex matrix structure.

6. DEVELOPING GLOBAL MARKETING STRATEGIES

The core decision is Standardization vs. Adaptation.

6.1 Product Strategies

  • Straight Extension: Marketing the same product without changes.
  • Product Adaptation: Changing the product to meet local conditions or preferences.
  • Product Invention: Creating something entirely new for a foreign market.

6.2 Promotion Strategies

  • Communication Adaptation: Using the same product but adapting the advertising message.
  • Dual Adaptation: Adapting both the product and the communication.
  • Standardized Global Advertising: Using the same message and visuals in all markets.

6.3 Pricing Strategies

  • Uniform Price: Setting the same price everywhere (rarely feasible).
  • Market-Based Price: Adjusting prices to match local competitive conditions and purchasing power.
  • Cost-Based Price: Adding a standard markup to the costs of product, shipping, and tariffs.

6.4 Place Strategies (Distribution)

This involves getting the product to the consumer in the foreign market. It requires a "whole-channel view," looking at the entire global value chain. Distribution systems vary vastly between countries.

7. MARKETING STRATEGIES OF MNCS IN INDIA

India presents unique challenges and opportunities for Multinational Corporations (MNCs):

  • Focus on Value (Cost Focus): High price sensitivity often requires downsizing products (e.g., smaller sachets) or low-cost manufacturing.
  • Rural Market Penetration: Using innovative distribution models and regional language advertising to reach a large rural population.
  • Digital Adoption: Investing heavily in digital marketing, e-commerce, and vernacular content.
  • Hybrid Products: Creating products that blend global quality standards with local cultural requirements (e.g., vegetarian-only lines).
  • Local Sourcing and Manufacturing ("Make in India"): Increasing the local footprint to gain government support and reduce import duties.
